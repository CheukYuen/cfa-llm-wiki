# Duration

## Definition

A measure of a fixed-income security's price sensitivity to interest rate changes, expressed as the weighted average time to receive its cash flows.

## Why it matters

Investors need a reliable way to quantify how much a bond’s price will fluctuate when market yields change. Duration solves this by translating complex cash flow schedules into a single, standardized risk metric.

This enables precise portfolio hedging, asset-liability matching, and comparative risk assessment across different securities. Without it, managing interest rate exposure would rely on guesswork rather than quantifiable sensitivity.

## Key points

- Macaulay duration calculates the weighted average time until cash flows are received.
- Modified duration estimates the percentage price change for a 1% shift in yield.
- Higher duration implies greater interest rate risk and price volatility.
- Portfolio duration is the market-value-weighted average of individual bond durations.

## Common confusions

- Mistaking duration for a bond’s simple time to maturity rather than a sensitivity measure.
- Assuming duration accurately predicts price changes for large yield shifts without convexity.
- Confusing Macaulay duration (time-based) with modified duration (price-sensitivity-based).
